Higher thresholds ≠ healthier markets. Penang set an RM3M floor, yet its Median Multiple remains 4.6 — local buyers have not benefited.

Johor Iskandar Case Study — Timeline

2006 Iskandar Malaysia established; state government mass-approved developments
2012 Foreign capital (mainly Chinese buyers) floods in
2013–2016 Mega-projects like Forest City launch; peak launches exceed 10,000 units/year
2016 China capital controls enacted; foreign demand collapses
2017–2018 Mass completions arrive; local demand cannot absorb
2018–present Persistent overhang: JB overhang at 3,293 units, 2nd highest nationally

State government over-approved supply; the demand base never supported these price points. When foreign capital receded, the problem surfaced.

5. Core Contradictions

Overhang Paradox

44,794 units unsold nationally.
Yet 53% of buyers cannot find suitable homes below RM300k.

Location Mismatch

Perak and Johor have the worst overhang.
Common thread: supply planning relied on external demand rather than local purchasing power.

Affordability Illusion

Government-defined affordable (≤RM300k) is only 18% of new launches, but 53% of actual transaction demand.
The gap is filled by PR1MA, PPR subsidised projects — private markets cannot self-correct.

Developer Dilemma

Land cost structure makes affordable housing financially irrational — unless government provides land subsidies or density bonuses.
This explains why the private market cannot solve the problem alone.

Much of KL's overhang consists of units that are "cheap in total price but expensive per sq ft." An RM280,000 apartment looks affordable — but at 280 sq ft, that's RM1,000 psf, more expensive than quality condos in KL suburbs.

5. Serviced Apartments — Structural Problem

Serviced apartments are legally commercial-use property. Developers enjoy lower land costs and fewer affordable housing quota requirements — a structural incentive for mass launches. But buyers bear higher utility rates and face tougher mortgage conditions.

6. Three KL Micro-Markets

KL is deeply fragmented internally. Same KL address, but KLCC corridor, Mid Valley corridor, and outer ring have completely different supply-demand dynamics.

Premium Core

KLCC / Bukit Bintang Corridor

Supply: Ultra-luxury (RM2M+), few new launches
Demand: Expats, MM2H, high-end local buyers
Status: New MM2H rules require RM600k–RM2M property purchase, directly boosting this zone's demand

Mid-Range Belt

Mid Valley / Old Klang Road Corridor

Supply: RM400k–RM800k mid-high condos, most active launches
Demand: Local M40–T20, expat professionals
Status: Most active 2024 launch price band; supply-demand relatively balanced

Outer Ring

Kepong / Cheras / Setapak

Supply: RM280k–RM500k mid-low condos, heavy serviced apartments
Demand: First-time buyers, B40–M40
Status: Worst overhang zone; PSF trap most concentrated here
